# Welcome Email Sequence

This is the 5-email welcome sequence for new Sunday Letter subscribers. Paste each email into Beehiiv's automation (see BEEHIIV-SETUP.md → Phase 4).

The goal of the sequence is not to "warm up" the subscriber. It is to deliver, immediately and repeatedly, the value the homepage promised. Each email gives them one piece of the manifesto content they have not yet seen, while reinforcing the editorial promise (no filler, no pity, one essay, every Sunday).

After Email 5, the subscriber enters the normal Sunday cadence — they get the regular weekly essay alongside everyone else.

---

## Email 1 — Welcome (Day 0, immediate)

**Subject:** You're in.

**Preview text:** What arrives, and why we wrote it this way.

**Body:**

You're in.

This is the welcome email for the Sunday Letter at AfterChildren — the unsparing weekly letter for parents whose children are grown. There is no confirmation hoop to jump through. You are subscribed. The next regular letter goes out this Sunday.

Before then, you will get four short emails from me over the next two weeks. Each one points you to one of the founding manifesto essays — the four cornerstone pieces that explain what this whole project is. After that, you are on the normal Sunday rhythm with everyone else.

Three things to know about what arrives in your inbox going forward:

1. **One essay, every Sunday morning.** 1,500 to 3,500 words on a single question that matters in the second half of life. Not a roundup. Not a digest. If I have nothing useful to say in a given week, I skip the week.

2. **No advertising in the body of the essay.** There may eventually be a small sponsor line, clearly labeled, at the top or bottom. The essay you are reading is the essay I wrote. Nobody paid to be in it.

3. **No pity. No hedging.** Most over-55 content is condescending or therapeutic. This letter is neither. I treat the reader as an adult who can handle direct information about money, body, mind, legal, and living decisions — and who is tired of being told what they want to hear.

If that is not for you, the unsubscribe link is at the bottom of every email. One click, no retention loop.

Tomorrow's email is the first manifesto essay, and the most important thing you can read on this site. Watch for it.

— M. Holloway
Founder, AfterChildren

P.S. If you want to skip ahead and read the four founding essays in order, they are linked at afterchildren.com/manifesto. They will also arrive in your inbox over the next two weeks if you'd rather pace it.

## Email 2 — The foundation (Day 1)

**Subject:** The framework underneath the whole site.

**Preview text:** Five things you have to build, in roughly this order.

**Body:**

Yesterday I said I would point you to the founding manifesto essays over the next two weeks. The first one is here.

**[You Raised Them. Now Build Yourself.](https://afterchildren.com/manifestos/you-raised-them-now-build-yourself)**

This is the essay underneath every other piece of writing on the site. It introduces the framework I use to organize the second half of life — the Five Sovereignties — and argues that they have to be built in roughly the right order, because some of them are foundations and some of them depend on the foundations being in place.

The order is:

1. **Body** — the foundation everything else sits on. Most people get this last; it should be first.
2. **Money** — the fuel. The retirement math has to actually work, not approximately work.
3. **Mind** — the architecture that holds the meaning. Built deliberately, not by default.
4. **Legal** — the walls. The protections most parents do not have.
5. **Living** — the roof. The decision with the most variance and the least examination.

The essay is 14 minutes. Read it when you have a quiet morning. The argument is not difficult; the implications are.

If, when you finish, you find yourself thinking "I have done almost none of this," welcome to the 80% of parents I am writing this letter for.

The next email in this sequence arrives in three days. It is the cornerstone financial essay — the hardest one I have written.

— M. Holloway

## Email 3 — The hardest essay (Day 4)

**Subject:** The hardest essay I've written.

**Preview text:** The quiet assumption baked into millions of retirement plans.

**Body:**

This is the most important email in the welcome sequence.

The essay below is the hardest one I have written for AfterChildren, because it asks the reader to give up a belief that has been comforting them, quietly, for years. The belief that their adult children will, in some form, be there to absorb the cost of aging.

For most middle-class parents in 2026, this belief is mathematically false. It does not match how the economy works, how adult children work, or how the long-term care system in the United States is structured. And the parents who plan around it as if it were true are setting up the most expensive surprise of their retirement.

**[The Hardest Truth: Your Adult Children Are Not Your Retirement Plan](https://afterchildren.com/manifestos/adult-children-not-retirement-plan)**

15 minutes. Numbers throughout. Four real strategies at the end. The deepest argument for doing the planning work is not financial — it is in the closing section, "The deepest reason to do this."

If you read one essay on this site, read this one. Forward it to a spouse if you have one. Forward it to a sibling if you are wrestling with these questions on behalf of an aging parent.

The next email in this sequence is about a separate but related decision — one that comes up at almost every family table eventually, and that gets made wrong in roughly 70% of cases. It arrives in three days.

— M. Holloway

## Email 4 — The 70% failure rate (Day 7)

**Subject:** The 70% failure rate nobody talks about.

**Preview text:** Why moving in with the kids almost always fails — and the alternative.

**Body:**

A week into the welcome sequence. One more manifesto essay and we are at the end of it.

This one is the most personally consequential decision on the site, for parents who are seriously considering it: whether to move in with adult children when aging becomes complicated.

**[Why Moving In With Your Kids Almost Always Fails](https://afterchildren.com/manifestos/moving-in-with-kids-fails)**

The peer-reviewed data on multigenerational households where adult children take in aging parents is consistent: about 70% of these arrangements are rated as significantly stressful or regretted within 24 months. Marriages strain. Parents lose identity. Adult children burn out. Almost nobody warns anyone about any of this, because the conversation is sealed behind a wall of love and obligation that nobody wants to break.

This essay breaks it. 14 minutes. Four failure modes, named with sources. Three patterns where it actually works. And — most importantly — the alternative most parents do not consider: move *near* the kids, not *into* them, while maintaining your own home and your own community.

You will recognize the situation if you have ever been in or near the conversation. The essay names what nobody at the family meeting says out loud.

One more email in the welcome sequence. It arrives in a week and is the shortest of the bunch. After that, you are on the regular Sunday cadence.

— M. Holloway

## Email 5 — The structural close (Day 14)

**Subject:** Welcome to Sunday.

**Preview text:** What happens from here, and how to get more out of the letter.

**Body:**

This is the last welcome email. From here, you are on the regular cadence — one long essay arrives on Sunday morning, every week, unless I genuinely have nothing useful to say (which happens about once a quarter).

Three small things, before we move into the normal rhythm:

**1. Replies are read.**

This is a one-person letter. I read every reply that comes to it. I do not respond to everyone individually — there is not enough time — but the most-asked questions become future essays. If a question is sitting on your mind that the four manifesto essays did not address, send it. "Money: ..." or "Legal: ..." or "Body: ..." in the subject line helps me sort.

**2. Forward freely.**

If a piece is useful to a friend, send it. The forward-to-a-friend dynamic is how this letter grows, and I would rather have the right readers arriving by referral than the wrong readers arriving from paid advertising. There is no referral program, no tracking, no incentive structure. Forward as a normal person would.

**3. Read the pillar pages when you have time.**

The five sovereignty pages on the site — Body, Money, Mind, Legal, Living — each list the questions I am working through over the coming months. The order in which articles arrive at the top of the Sunday Letter is partly driven by what readers ask for. The pillar pages tell you what is queued.

That's it. The next thing in your inbox is a normal Sunday essay.

— M. Holloway

P.S. If the letter is not earning its place in your inbox, unsubscribe in one click at the bottom of any email. No retention loop. No survey. I do not take it personally — the wrong newsletter at the wrong time is worse than no newsletter at all.

## Open rate targets (for tracking)

- Email 1 (welcome): >65% open rate is normal for an immediate welcome
- Emails 2–5: 35–50% target. Below 30% suggests the subject lines need rework.

If a subject line consistently underperforms (especially Email 3 or 4 — the heaviest sells), test alternates. Suggested A/B variants:

- Email 3 alt: "The retirement assumption you should give up."
- Email 4 alt: "Why moving in with the kids fails 70% of the time."

Do not change the body copy without thinking carefully — the editorial voice is doing real work in retention.
